The Indian space sector, with nearly 30% of its companies in manufacturing, may find a promising solution to bolster its workforce. The government is contemplating various measures to enhance apprenticeship training across millions of micro, small, and medium enterprises (MSMEs) in India, aiming to cultivate a skilled workforce.

Proposed initiatives include reducing apprenticeship tenure from six to three months, increasing stipend subsidies for MSMEs to 50%, providing tax incentives for hiring apprentices, and engaging the private sector to address staff shortages faced by MSMEs, as per a senior government official familiar with the discussions. Depending on fiscal considerations, the government may amend existing apprenticeship schemes or formulate new ones tailored to MSMEs.

These enterprises constitute 38.4% of total manufacturing output, contribute 45.03% to total exports, and are vital to India's economic fabric. With ongoing dialogues with industry stakeholders, the government aims to unveil a comprehensive plan for MSME apprenticeship hiring by mid-year. India's vast MSME sector comprises an estimated 64 million units, employing 23% of the workforce and contributing 27% to GDP.

The Indian Space Research Organisation (ISRO) successfully conducted its third mission involving the Reusable Launch Vehicle (RLV), named "Pushpak" after the legendary spaceship from the Ramayan. Marking a significant stride towards democratizing space access. ISRO Chairperson S Somanath underscored the importance of this endeavor, hailing Pushpak as India's bold initiative to render space access more affordable.

Central to the innovation is the Reusable Launch Vehicle (RLV) concept, wherein the upper stage, housing crucial electronics, is designed for safe return to Earth, paving the way for cost-effective missions. This pioneering technology holds promise for in-orbit satellite refueling and refurbishment, crucial for mitigating space debris

Designed as a fully reusable single-stage-to-orbit (SSTO) vehicle, Pushpak integrates advanced elements from prior demonstrators, heralding a new era in India's space exploration. With its sleek design featuring fuselage, delta wings,vertical tails,and active control surfaces.

NASA plans to test new lidar technologies from an aircraft this summer, aiding Earth science remote sensing and potentially improving Moon mapping for Artemis landings. Lidars use laser beams to measure distances and provide 3D imaging, aiding navigation and data collection. Engineers at NASA’s Goddard Space Flight Center are refining lidars for lighter, smaller designs with input from small businesses and academia.

The Concurrent Artificially Intelligent Spectrometry and Adaptive Lidar System (CASALS) offers enhanced capabilities for precise landings. CASALS, funded by NASA’s Earth Science Technology Office, will undergo testing in 2024, bringing it closer to spaceflight readiness.
